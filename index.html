Fine-Grained Car Make Classification

87.55% top-1 on CompCars, 4.65 points over the OverFeat baseline

Five CNN architectures compared on 30,955 images across 75 makes and 431 models, from a CNN trained from scratch through ResNet50, EfficientNet-B0, and a Squeeze-and-Excitation variant, up to a hierarchical multi-task model with a shared backbone and dual heads. The auxiliary model-recognition task acts as a regularizer on the make head. EfficientNet-B0 reaches 844 images per second against ResNet50's 411, at a sixth of the parameters.

PyTorch, torchvision, CUDA, transfer learning, attention, multi-task learning

Weather Classification with Engineered Features and SVM

96.04% on MWD, and the descriptor transfers to ACDC at 90.6%

No deep learning. Hand-engineered colour and Difference-of-Gaussian descriptors, 1562 dimensions reduced to 200 by PCA, and a GridSearch-tuned SVM. The interesting part is the cross-dataset study: the same feature design, carried to a differently distributed dataset with the classifier refit, holds up at 90.6%, which quantifies what a colour-specialised descriptor is actually worth off its home turf. The fitted models ship, so the classifier runs without downloading anything.

scikit-learn, OpenCV, PCA, SVM, feature engineering

Deep Reinforcement Learning for Snake

196% over the heuristic baseline, and the blind agent beats the sighted one

PPO agents on a custom Gymnasium environment, compared under full and partial observability and across three reward designs. The agent restricted to a 5x5 window around its own head outperformed the agent that could see the whole board, and it is the only one that ever completes a board. The step-penalty variant is a clean demonstration of reward hacking: integrated over the horizon, the penalty exceeded two fruit rewards, so dying immediately became optimal, and that is exactly what the agent learned.

PyTorch, Stable-Baselines3, Gymnasium, PPO, reward shaping, CUDA

Sunflower: a Two-Axis Solar Tracker

Built from scratch, from the gear teeth to the control law

A solar-tracking robot made almost entirely from parts already in storage. Printed body and spur gearbox designed in Fusion and OpenSCAD, custom electronics around an Arduino Nano and an L298N H-bridge, and a closed-loop controller that climbs the gradient of measured luminosity, because the sensor has no fixed setpoint to regulate against and a PID has nothing to hold. The first gearbox stalled the motors and the analogue front end failed under soldering. Both failures are in the report, with what they forced.

Arduino, C++, control theory, Autodesk Fusion, OpenSCAD, FDM, sensor calibration
